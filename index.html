I

Title & Introduction

Give the “Empire and National Identity” readings a unifying title and explain their shared importance.

Guidance: Invent a single, creative title that could sit on the front of your MLA document or slides. In 1–2 focused paragraphs, explain how these texts together explore empire, nationhood, and identity—for both colonizers and colonized.
II

Whom Did You Read?

Identify the authors and works in the “Empire and National Identity” cluster.

Guidance: For each assigned piece in this cluster, list: author, full title, the author’s dates and location (as available), the original publication context (journal, book, year), and a 2–3 sentence summary focused on empire and national identity.
III

First / “Gut” Reactions

Capture each group member’s first impressions of the empire/nation themes.

Guidance: Do not analyze here—record feelings and first responses. Name each group member and briefly describe their initial reaction to the “Empire and National Identity” readings: anger at injustice, admiration for resistance, discomfort with imperial attitudes, confusion, surprise, etc. Then note whether those reactions changed after discussion.
All group members must appear here.
IV

Formal Aspects of the Texts

Identify genres, rhetorical modes, and key literary techniques used to represent empire and nationhood.

Guidance: For each work in this cluster, name the genre (poetry, prose, speech, essay, etc.) and the dominant rhetorical mode (narration, description, compare/contrast, argument, etc.). Use specific quotations or details to show how figurative language, imagery, symbolism, or irony shape our understanding of empire and national identity.
V

Scope & Content

Clarify the historical context and main ideas about empire and identity.

Guidance: Explain the historical and cultural background of British imperial expansion and debates over national identity in the period your texts come from. Then answer: What specifically are these texts about? How do they present empire—as civilizing, destructive, inevitable, resistible? What versions of “nation” or “home” do they imagine?
VI

Style & Voice

Compare how different authors speak about empire and national identity.

Guidance: Who is speaking in each text—a colonizer, a colonial subject, a narrator looking back? What point of view do they use? Are the sentences formal, official, and confident, or broken, ironic, and questioning? Compare voices that defend empire with those that resist it, using short examples to show tone.
VII

Audience

Think about who each empire-related text is written for.

Guidance: Based on style, vocabulary, and original publication context, who seems to be the intended audience? British politicians, educated middle-class readers, colonial administrators, colonized subjects, fellow activists? Was each piece widely read in its own time, or did it become important later? How does the intended audience shape how empire and national identity are described?
VIII

Most Notable Aspects

Name what most surprised or disturbed you about representations of empire and identity.

Guidance: Identify anything shocking, ironic, or powerful in the way these texts talk about empire, race, nationhood, or “civilization.” Are there images, metaphors, or claims you found difficult to accept? If nothing felt controversial, explain why the works seemed straightforward or reassuring in their picture of empire.
IX

Best Vocabulary

Collect powerful or revealing words about empire, race, and nationhood.

Guidance: After highlighting in your anthology, choose especially “great” words that help build ideas about empire and national identity. These might be technical terms, political slogans, or loaded adjectives. For each term, give the word, a brief definition in your own words, and why it matters in this context.
X

Connections & Associations

Connect the “Empire and National Identity” readings to films, art, music, and other literature.

Guidance: Brainstorm as a group. Which movies, songs, paintings, TV shows, novels, plays, or poems feel connected to the way these texts present empire and national identity? Be selective and explain the link: similar portrayals of colonial subjects, parallel struggles over belonging, or contrasting views of national pride and shame.
