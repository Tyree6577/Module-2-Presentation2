01 — Introduction

Reading the Empire

Empire is not merely a political structure — it is a cultural, psychological, and literary force. The readings assigned to our group explore how imperial power shapes national consciousness, how colonized peoples negotiate their identities under domination, and how writers have used literature as both a mirror and a weapon against empire.

From the confident rhetoric of Rudyard Kipling's imperialism to the subversive counter-narratives of Chinua Achebe and Frantz Fanon, these texts reveal a fundamental tension: empire creates national identity for some while erasing it for others.

Our analysis focuses on three key threads: the mythology of civilizing missions, the violence of imposed identity, and the literary response of the colonized. Each thread illuminates how literature both perpetuates and resists imperial ideologies.

"The Empire is under the world's orders, and the world is under the Empire's orders."
— Paraphrased colonial logic, 19th century

"Nations are not born. They are made — and unmade — by those who hold the pen as well as the sword."
— Literary Criticism Tradition
02 — Background

Historical & Political Context

To understand the readings, one must understand the world that produced them. The age of European empire — roughly 1500–1960 — reshaped the globe's political geography and its cultural imagination. Literature was entangled with empire from the start: travel narratives justified conquest; novels celebrated the colonial hero; poetry made empire feel noble.

1500s–1700s

Age of Exploration & Early Colonialism

European powers establish colonies across the Americas, Africa, and Asia. Literature begins framing indigenous peoples as "savage" and European intervention as civilizing. Shakespeare's The Tempest (1611) encodes this anxious dynamic through Caliban and Prospero.

1800s

High Imperialism & National Literature

The 19th century saw empire reach its zenith. Kipling's "The White Man's Burden" (1899) made imperial ideology poetic. Meanwhile, nationalist literatures emerged in colonized territories — writers like Tagore in India began articulating a counter-identity rooted in local culture, not British values.

1950s–1960s

Decolonization & Anti-Imperial Writing

As formal empires collapsed, a new wave of writers confronted their aftermath. Chinua Achebe's Things Fall Apart (1958) directly challenged the Conradian vision of Africa. Frantz Fanon's The Wretched of the Earth (1961) argued that colonialism was a psychological as much as a political wound.

1970s–Today

Postcolonial Theory & National Identity Debates

Edward Said's Orientalism (1978) gave scholars a vocabulary for critiquing how Western literature constructed the "Other." The conversation continues: who gets to define a nation's identity? Whose stories are told? These questions animate contemporary literature from across the formerly colonized world.

03 — Contextual Awareness

Why Context Matters

Reading these texts without their historical context risks misreading them entirely. A passage that seems like adventure writing may in fact encode racial hierarchy. A heroic narrative may depend on the erasure of other people's stories. Here are four reasons context is essential:

01

Language is Political

The vocabulary of empire — "civilization," "barbarian," "progress" — was not neutral. Understanding how these terms functioned historically reveals the ideological work literature was doing, even in texts that appear to be "just stories."

02

Whose Perspective Dominates?

Most canonical imperial texts are narrated from the colonizer's perspective. Recognizing this allows readers to hear what is suppressed or distorted — the silenced voices whose presence haunts the margins of the text.

03

National Identity is Constructed

Nations present themselves as natural, ancient, and inevitable. Historically aware reading reveals that national identities are constructed — through literature, education, maps, and cultural policy — often in service of power.

04

Literature Responds to Literature

Many postcolonial texts are direct responses to earlier imperial ones. Achebe's Things Fall Apart cannot be fully understood without Camus, Conrad, and Joyce. The literary conversation across empire is essential context.

04 — Drawbacks & Resistance

Society's Failures & Who Pushed Back

Empire did not benefit everyone — not even those it claimed to elevate. Its ideological structures created profound injustices that writers, thinkers, and activists worked to name and dismantle.


  • ▸Cultural Erasure. Imperial education systems actively suppressed indigenous languages, religions, and knowledge systems. In Nigeria, India, the Caribbean, and beyond, colonial schooling taught children that their own cultures were inferior. Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o's memoir Decolonising the Mind (1986) documents this psychological violence — and his decision to stop writing in English.
  • ▸Economic Exploitation Masked as Progress. The "civilizing mission" provided ideological cover for extraction. Raw materials left colonized lands; wealth accumulated in European capitals. Walter Rodney's How Europe Underdeveloped Africa (1972) systematically documents this reversal of the "progress" narrative.
  • ▸Racial Hierarchy Codified in Law. Imperial legal systems institutionalized racial inequality. The apartheid regime in South Africa, Jim Crow laws in America, and caste-based discrimination across colonial South Asia all drew on imperial racial science — pseudoscience that literature often uncritically reproduced.
  • ▸Women Doubly Marginalized. Imperial national identity was typically masculine. Women in colonized territories faced both colonial and patriarchal oppression. Writers like Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie and Jean Rhys (in Wide Sargasso Sea) reclaimed female subjectivity from imperial narratives.
  • ▸Resistance and Response. The people did push back. The Harlem Renaissance created a counter-canon of Black American literature. The Négritude movement, led by Aimé Césaire and Léopold Sédar Senghor, celebrated African culture as a response to European denigration. Literary resistance was political resistance.
05 — Textual Evidence

Readings as Proof

The following passages from our class readings demonstrate the tensions of empire and national identity. Each excerpt is followed by our analytical commentary.

Rudyard Kipling — "The White Man's Burden" (1899)
"Take up the White Man's burden / Send forth the best ye breed / Go bind your sons to exile / To serve your captives' need."
Analysis: Kipling frames imperialism as a noble sacrifice — the colonizer serving the colonized. This paternalistic inversion is central to imperial ideology: it erases the violence of conquest by recasting it as altruism. Note that the colonized are described as "captives," inadvertently admitting the coercive reality the poem's language otherwise obscures. The poem constructs a national identity for Britain built on duty, sacrifice, and racial superiority — all simultaneously.
Chinua Achebe — "An Image of Africa: Racism in Conrad's Heart of Darkness" (1977)
"The question is whether a novel which celebrates [the] dehumanization... of African people can be called a great work of art."
Analysis: Achebe's challenge to Conrad is itself a literary act — a colonized writer reclaiming the authority to judge which texts deserve canonical status. His argument is not that Conrad is a bad writer, but that literary greatness cannot be cleanly separated from moral vision. This essay shows how the postcolonial response to empire often takes the form of critical rereadings that expose the hidden assumptions of supposedly "universal" masterpieces.
Frantz Fanon — The Wretched of the Earth (1961)
"The colonized man finds his freedom in and through violence... it frees the native from his inferiority complex and from his despair and inaction."
Analysis: Fanon's controversial claim about violence must be read in its context: he is describing a psychological dynamic, not issuing a moral prescription. For Fanon, colonial humiliation is so thorough that the colonized subject can only reconstitute a sense of agency by refusing the colonizer's terms of engagement. This passage illuminates why national liberation movements often took the form they did — and why Fanon remains contested. His insight is that national identity after colonialism cannot simply be restored; it must be rebuilt.
